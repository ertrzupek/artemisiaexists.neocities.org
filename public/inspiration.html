Game Inspiration and Devlog for Stuck In Irons

March 13th, 2026 - Introduction

I have yet to find a video game that labels itself as both cozy and horror. I mean, what does that even entail? You get scared, but you have fun doing it? You feel comforted by the horrors occurring all around you? There’s many ways such a strange scenario could play out, and I plan to make such a game to see what I can do with such an esoteric genre mashup. In a different vein, there are many games that use themes of horror and water, or horror and fishing, or horror and sailing. Sailing is a pretty cozy thing to do, when all the conditions are right: plot the course, anchor up, sails down, and hope you don’t get stuck in irons. Being stuck in irons is when the wind is going directly towards you, so there’s no forward lift and you’re just unable to move. So thats my game. A cozy horror sailing game where you plot your course to different points of interest, direct your crew with various deck commands, and explore your boat as its captain. Only, your electronics have stopped working, and you’re stuck on the open ocean with no lines of communication and no land for miles. This is Stuck in Irons.

Let’s back up first and describe how this game idea even came about. I’m a student game designer with years of experience in both major game engines, Unity and Unreal. I’m made many personal and group projects, but I haven’t put the time in to officially release a game yet. On a whim, I decided to sign up for a seamanship and navigation course with my friends this past semester. There is so much to learn about sailing and its history, but what really caught my attention was the role of the fiddler. Bear with me. The fiddler (or general musician) aboard large vessels would play songs that would match different deck commands from the captain. Slower paced songs would imply a sustained effort, like raising and lowering the sails, while a faster upbeat song asks for rapid motions, like pulling lines. This fascinated me, and I immediately wanted to figure out how to gamify this system. Additionally, the entire system of navigation by both stars and physical charts was so engaging for me, and I’m now highly invested in making a similar system with the same tactile and satisfying feel. And there was the birth of the idea I’m now calling Stuck in Irons (kudos to my friend for coming up with that one).

But what actually is this writing for? I’ve always been a big fan of atmospheric horror games, where your imagination and fears are your own worst enemy. Some games that inspire me are Iron Lung and Voices of the Void, both of which do atmospheric horror fantastically in different ways. However, I’ve never played any sailing games in my life, and it’s very hard to build off of a genre if I don’t know what’s already out there. Therefore, I’ve started this series to discuss various games that I have played or will be playing as market and design research. Each entry will contain a summary/review of the game, its main mechanic(s) of interest, and what I will be twisting/innovating on. And just for funsies, I’ll also talk a bit about the features of the main sailboat in the game, or my favorite if there is more than one to choose from.
